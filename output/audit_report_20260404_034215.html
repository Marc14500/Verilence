Operator Override Calculation and Payment Contradiction

What We Found

Section 4.4: From Net Revenue, the following allocations are made: ... (B) Operator's Revenue Override: 2.5% of GROSS revenue (before deductions), separate from WI share vs Section 4.3: After deductions (1)-(5), remaining amount = 'Net Revenue to Joint Account' Problem: There is a direct contradiction in the accounting waterfall. Section 4.3 defines 'Net Revenue' as what remains *after* deductions like royalties and taxes are paid from Gross Revenue. Section 4.4 then states the Operator's Override is an allocation made *from* this Net Revenue. However, it simultaneously dictates the override is calculated as 2.5% of *Gross Revenue*. This is financially and logically inconsistent. An amount calculated on the pre-deduction 'Gross' figure cannot be an allocation 'from' the post-deduction 'Net' figure without creating a significant accounting conflict. This ambiguity determines whether the override is effectively a project cost borne by all (reducing the Net Revenue pot) or a distribution of profit paid to the Operator.

Assignment Consent Threshold Ambiguity

What We Found

Article 5, Section 2: No Party may assign its Working Interest without consent of all other Parties. vs Article 5, Section 1: Simple majority (>50%) required for most decisions; supermajority (>60%) required for Operator removal. Problem: The agreement creates an ambiguity between a specific provision and a general one. The assignment clause requires unanimous consent ('consent of all other Parties'), which is a 100% voting threshold. However, the general voting provision states that 'most decisions' only require a simple majority (>50%). It is unclear whether a vote on an assignment is one of the 'most decisions' governed by the majority rule, or if the specific unanimity requirement for assignments supersedes the general rule. This ambiguity would lead to a dispute if a Party wishes to sell its interest and can secure >50% approval but not 100%.

Unresolved Disproportionate Royalty Burden

What We Found

Pioneer bears disproportionate cost exposure on higher-royalty acreage, which is not explicitly addressed in this Agreement. Problem: The agreement's core financial mechanism (pooling all costs and allocating by a single Working Interest percentage) is in direct conflict with the underlying reality of the assets, where royalty costs are not uniform. By acknowledging the issue but stating it is 'not explicitly addressed,' the contract codifies an unresolved inconsistency. Pioneer could argue that the simple allocation method in 1.3 is inequitable and conflicts with the acknowledged reality in 4.5, leading to a dispute over the proper allocation of royalty costs.

Impossible 'Sole Risk' Definition

What We Found

Section 1.6: Sole Risk = Well drilled by Operator at Operator's sole risk and cost; Non-Operators have zero obligation to contribute costs or bear liability. vs Section 1.2: As of the Restatement Date (January 15, 2020), the Working Interest percentages are: Renaissance Resources LP: 0% (Operator, holds no Working Interest but operates for commission) Problem: The definition of a 'Sole Risk' operation is functionally impossible under the terms of this agreement. An operator with a 0% Working Interest has no capital at risk and does not share in costs. Therefore, it is impossible for the Operator to drill a well at its 'sole risk and cost.' This definition appears to be boilerplate from a standard JOA and was not amended to reflect the unique commercial arrangement. This creates a contractual void; if Non-Operators vote down an AFE, the Operator's primary recourse in a standard JOA (proposing a sole risk well) is unavailable, which could lead to operational deadlock.

Inappropriate Operator Authority to Purchase Third-Party Hydrocarbons

What We Found

Section 2.1(a)(iv): Operator shall have full, exclusive authority to conduct all operations within the Contract Area. Operations include but are not limited to: ... (iv) purchasing crude oil, natural gas, and condensate from third parties if economically justified; vs Section 1.2: Renaissance Resources LP: 0% (Operator, holds no Working Interest but operates for commission)... NOTE: Renaissance's lack of Working Interest creates a structural conflict of interest in the Operator role. Problem: This clause grants the Operator, who has no working interest and thus no financial stake in the profitability of the Joint Account, the authority to use Joint Account funds for commodity speculation. This is fundamentally inconsistent with the purpose of a JOA, which is to govern the joint development of the parties' own mineral assets. The Operator's incentive is to maximize activity and gross revenue (to increase fees and its override), not to maximize profit. Allowing it to purchase third-party oil with Non-Operator funds, based on its own subjective determination of what is 'economically justified', creates an extreme and unbounded financial risk for the Non-Operators.
