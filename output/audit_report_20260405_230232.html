Invalid Cross-Reference to Non-Existent Article for Default Security Rights

What We Found

7.4.B. Remedies: "If any party fails to pay its share of the expense when due, the other party or parties paying the defaulting party's share shall be entitled to receive the share of production of the defaulting party until such time as the proceeds from the sale of such share (after deducting all marketing costs and all royalty, overriding royalty, and production payments) shall equal the total of the unpaid amount plus interest at the rate of ten percent (10%) per annum. In addition, each party so paying its share shall be subrogated to the security rights described in Article VII.B of this agreement." vs Document-wide: The document does not contain an "Article VII.B". Article VII is divided into numbered sections (7.1, 7.2, 7.3, 7.4), but there is no sub-article designated as 'B'. Problem: This is a critical scrivener's error. The clause attempts to grant non-defaulting parties a security interest (likely a lien) against the defaulting party's interest, which is the primary mechanism for recovering unpaid funds. By referencing a non-existent article, the intended security interest is likely void and unenforceable. This forces the non-defaulting parties to rely on more costly and time-consuming remedies like litigation, and they may lose their priority status to other creditors in a bankruptcy scenario.

Inconsistent Standards for Operator's Performance vs. Liability

What We Found

5.1. Designation and Responsibilities of Operator: "Operator... shall conduct... all such operations in a good and workmanlike manner, as a reasonably prudent operator would..." vs 5.1. Designation and Responsibilities of Operator: "...but it shall have no liability as Operator to the other parties for losses sustained or liabilities incurred, except such as may result from gross negligence or from breach of the provisions of this agreement." Problem: The agreement establishes a duty for the Operator to perform to an ordinary negligence standard ('reasonably prudent operator') but then immediately exculpates the Operator from liability for anything less than gross negligence. This creates a 'right without a remedy' for the Non-Operators. They can prove the Operator acted imprudently and caused a loss, but they cannot recover damages unless they meet the much higher and more difficult-to-prove standard of gross negligence. This can lead to disputes where Non-Operators bear the cost of the Operator's simple but costly mistakes.

Ambiguous Notice Period for Preferential Right to Purchase

What We Found

8.5. Preferential Right to Purchase: "The other parties shall have a period of ten (10) days after receipt of the notice to elect to purchase..." vs Article XI. NOTICES: "...notices... shall be deemed to have been properly served... after 72 hours from the time they are deposited in the United States mail..." Problem: A conflict exists between when the 10-day election period begins. Section 8.5 states it starts upon "receipt" of the notice, which implies actual, physical receipt. However, Article XI creates a legal fiction where notice is "deemed served" 72 hours after mailing, regardless of when it actually arrives. A party could miscalculate its deadline, believing they have 10 days from when the mail arrives, when the selling party might argue the clock started 72 hours after they mailed it. This ambiguity could cause a party to inadvertently lose its valuable right to purchase.

Ambiguous Agreement Term upon Renewal of All Original Leases

What We Found

Article X. TERM OF AGREEMENT: "This agreement shall remain in full force and effect for the primary term of the Leases and so long as any of the Leases described in Exhibit 'A' are maintained in force..." vs 8.3. Renewal or Extension of Leases: "If any party secures a renewal or replacement of any Lease subject hereto, it shall promptly notify all other parties... Each other party... [may] elect to participate in the ownership of the renewal..." Problem: The JOA's term is explicitly tied to the continued existence of the original leases listed in Exhibit 'A'. If the last of these original leases expires, but a party immediately secures a renewal lease under Article 8.3, it is ambiguous whether the JOA continues to govern. A renewal lease is technically a new contract and not one of the leases "described in Exhibit 'A'". A party wishing to exit the JOA could argue that the agreement automatically terminated upon the expiration of the last original lease, voiding key provisions like the Operatorship and preferential rights for the new leasehold.
